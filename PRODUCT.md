# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Parents of children aged 1–4 years living near the Zona Norte (El Remanso) or Urubó (Villa Bonita) areas of Santa Cruz de la Sierra, Bolivia. They arrive from Instagram, Facebook, TikTok, WhatsApp referrals, or word of mouth — already aware they need a daycare, already anxious about leaving their young child somewhere for the first time. They are not looking for a lecture on Montessori pedagogy; they are looking for evidence that this specific place is safe, trustworthy, and right for their child.

Secondary audience: grandparents or relatives who research options on behalf of the family.

## Product Purpose

Casita Montessori Santa Cruz is a Montessori daycare for children from walking age (~1 year) through pre-kinder (~4 years), with two locations in Santa Cruz de la Sierra. The product is a one-page website whose job is to resolve parental anxiety and convert it into a WhatsApp conversation. Success means: a parent who landed with the question "Will my child be safe and happy here?" leaves with enough evidence to say yes, and taps the WhatsApp button.

The site is not a brochure. It is not a school website. It is a trust-building conversion page that answers the right questions in the right order.

## Brand Personality

Warm, honest, and unhurried. Casita Montessori speaks like a person who has cared for hundreds of small children and knows exactly what parents worry about — and addresses those worries calmly, concretely, and without overselling. The tone is **direct** (no "perhaps consider"), **grounded in specifics** (cameras, schedules, routines, real ages), and **warm without being saccharine** (no cutesy language, no princess-and-superhero imagery, no promises that exceed reality).

Three-word personality: **close, honest, caring**.

The brand sits between a trusted neighbor and a thoughtful educator. Not a corporate institution. Not a generic "fun" daycare. A place that feels like a well-run home.

## Anti-references

The site and brand must avoid everything that erodes trust or misrepresents the service:

- **Generic daycare aesthetics**: bright primary colors everywhere, cartoon animals, Disney characters, princesses, superheroes, rainbow palettes.
- **Premium/luxury signaling**: marble textures, gold accents, "best in the city" language, premium-school vibes the physical space cannot deliver.
- **Bilingual school framing**: English-language claims, "bilingual" positioning, international curriculum language — Casita does not offer this and must not imply it.
- **Oversized promises**: "100% safe", "guaranteed development", "personalized attention at all times", "best daycare in Santa Cruz".
- **Technical Montessori jargon overload**: citing Montessori theory at length instead of showing what it looks like in daily practice.
- **Corporate coldness**: stock photos, formal institutional language, impersonal website templates.
- **Hedging language**: "might", "could", "perhaps", "consider". Every claim should be a fact or a concrete commitment.

## Design Principles

1. **Trust before method.** Safety and visibility (live cameras, controlled pickup, adapted spaces) are the primary conversion triggers. Montessori is important, but it supports the trust case — it does not lead it.
2. **Concrete over abstract.** "Children wash their hands, put on their crocs, choose their activity" beats "we promote autonomy." Show the day. Show the routine. Show the detail.
3. **Warmth through restraint.** Natural materials, warm neutrals, real-life textures (wood, clay, linen) communicate Montessori and calm without resorting to childish color explosions. The palette is for the parents, not the children.
4. **One clear action.** Every section points toward the same outcome: open WhatsApp and ask about availability. Primary CTA is always "Consultar cupos por WhatsApp." Secondary is "Agendar una visita."
5. **Answer before they ask.** The 15 most common parent questions (age range, schedules, cameras, pickup policy, potty training, snacks, enrollment) must be answered on the page — either in section copy or the FAQ — so the WhatsApp conversation starts with intent, not with information-gathering.

## Copy Voice

Use plain, direct Spanish. Speak to the parent in second person ("tu hijo", "tus dudas"). Short sentences. No subjunctive hedging. No grandiose claims.

**Approved formulations:**
- "Sabemos que dejar a tu hijo pequeño en una guardería es una decisión importante."
- "Los padres pueden acceder a cámaras en vivo para ver cómo están sus hijos durante la jornada."
- "Acompañamos cada rutina: higiene, merienda, orden, juego y salida."
- "No hacemos todo por ellos; los acompañamos para que descubran que pueden hacerlo."

**Avoid:**
- "El mejor lugar para tu hijo."
- "Educación de calidad." (too generic)
- "Tu hijo será feliz aquí." (unverifiable)
- Any claim not backed by a specific practice or protocol.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Baseline: WCAG 2.1 AA. Key commitments:
- Color contrast verified against actual values, not eyeballed.
- All interactive elements keyboard-accessible with visible focus states.
- `prefers-reduced-motion` respected for every animation.
- Semantic HTML; ARIA only where semantic HTML is insufficient.
- Copy in plain Spanish at a clear reading level; Montessori terms explained in parentheses when used ("trabajo libre (los niños eligen su actividad)").
- Images of children must include descriptive `alt` text focused on the activity, not the child.
